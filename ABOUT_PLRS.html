About the Public Legal Record System (PLRS)

The Public Legal Record System (PLRS) is an open, cryptographically verifiable record system designed to eliminate silent edits, hidden revisions, and discretionary report control in legal and protective processes.

Why PLRS Exists

In many modern systems, critical legal records can be:
  • Edited after the fact
  • Rewritten without visible trace
  • Reinterpreted across time
  • Restricted behind closed institutional access

PLRS was created to solve one specific problem:

Once a record is created, it should never be able to disappear or silently change.

What Makes PLRS Different

Unlike traditional databases or case management systems, PLRS:
  • Uses cryptographic hashes to lock records permanently
  • Operates on append-only public indices
  • Allows challenges without overwriting original records
  • Separates identity from public truth through anonymization
  • Allows anyone to independently verify integrity

What PLRS Is Not

PLRS does not:
  • Decide guilt or innocence
  • Replace judges, attorneys, or courts
  • Make moral or legal rulings
  • Expose identities without consent
It exists only to preserve the integrity of records.

Who Can Use PLRS

PLRS can be used by:
  • Private citizens
  • Journalists
  • Attorneys and legal teams
  • Advocacy organizations
  • Courts and oversight bodies
No permission, account, or institutional affiliation is required to verify records.

Verification Over Trust

PLRS is built on a single principle:

You should never be required to trust a platform, authority, or institution to know whether a record was altered.

If the hashes match, the record is authentic. If they do not, the tampering is provable.

Public Accountability

By making records publicly verifiable rather than privately controlled, PLRS shifts accountability away from discretionary power and toward mathematical proof.
